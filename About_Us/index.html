The Strategy

In 1790, Benjamin Franklin left 1,000 pounds in his will to be invested for 100 years before dispersal. By the end of the century, the fund had grown to over $2 million—a testament to the quiet power of compound interest and long-term vision.

Today, mycology is in a precarious position. Despite its central role in ecosystems, medicine, and sustainability, fungal science receives limited attention and even less funding. Research is beholden to institutions with short-term incentives. We believe the best way to secure our scientific future is the fungal way: slow, deliberate, and intergenerational.

The Sclerotia Project emulates this approach. We aim to gather a modest initial sum—$5,000—and invest it in a total stock market index fund with a long-term average return of ~6.5% annually. Left untouched for 100 years, that investment could grow to over $2.7 million:

FV = $5,000 × (1 + 0.065)100 ≈ $2,716,006

Source: SEC Compound Interest Calculator

This is not a traditional fundraiser. It is a grassroots, decentralized endowment seeded by mycologists themselves. Future generations will inherit the fund.

By contributing a small amount today, we become ancestors of a fungal legacy. Like sclerotia, we store energy for a time to come. The best time to start this was 100 years ago. The second-best time is now.
